Description

In this class, we will explore some of the findings, theories, and methods in the field of developmental psychology. We will start the course considering some big questions: How is knowledge acquired? How do infants think about and perceive the world around them? What do they know in infancy, and how do they learn the abstract concepts that mature adults eventually come to possess? How does the environment -- caregivers, friends, culture -- influence the way children think? Importantly, these are all empirical questions, and during this course we will focus on how we can get developmental data to bear on these and other questions we care about. We will also explore how developmental data can bear on other broader issues in cognitive psychology.

Aims

By the end of this course, you should be able to:
  • understand current research methods in the field
  • be familiar with some major theories of cognitive development
  • learn how to read developmental research articles critically
  • learn how to bring data to bear on applied and theoretical questions

Course format

In the first week, we will cover some general topics: why study development? What questions are developmental psychologists interested in? How do they study those questions? What theories guide research in the field?

We will have three classes each week. In each class, we will focus on a case study. We will read an article that bears on the topic of the day. Some of these will be articles reporting studies; others will be theoretical or review articles. Our close to three hours of class time will be spent on lectures and discussion (these *really* require doing the assigned readings).

Assignments

In-class participation (15%): There will be many opportunities to engage in discussion each week. Your participation grade depends on making some contribution to class discussion. Making good contributions requires doing the readings, so please read the required articles thoroughly and come prepared to talk about them. AI could help you understand articles better at home when you're reading them, but AI cannot help you in an in-class discussion. Read the articles yourselves. You're not graded on correctness. The most important thing is to try to grasp the ideas yourself and bring them to class to discuss.

In-class presentations (35%): You will need to present a response to three of the articles we are scheduled to read in class. Do not summarize the paper in your responses. Instead, take for granted that your classmates (and us) have read and understood the paper, and develop a response to the article, such as a criticism or an extension. These should be about 5-7 minutes long.

Your criticisms cannot be that the sample was too small or sampled out of convenience. Collecting data is tough, and one imperfect sample is motivation for some other researcher to replicate the study with a different sample. Instead, engage with the paper more deeply -- does their argument work? Did they measure what they wanted to? Suppose you now believe everything that the researchers argued for. Now what? What are the next interesting scientific questions that can be answered? Is there a part of the paper that you think could be extended in theoretically interesting ways?

Questions (15%): For each article that you are not presenting a response for, you will need to submit a question or comment. These are due the night before the article is being discussed in class. These can be clarifications, but they can also be genuine questions about the theory or methods.

Final project (35%): For the final project, you will write up a proposal for a study. The study can be on any area of development. There isn't any strict format for this, but your writeup should roughly follow IMRaD format. It should include: (1) an introduction that is long enough to motivate the problem you plan to study, (2) the methods you will use to study your question, including sample, materials, and procedure, (3) predicted results, and (4) a discussion of the significance of your study. We will provide you with an example of a writeup.

A part of the grade for this final project (10 of the 35%) will include an in-class presentation on the last two days of class (June 29 and 30).

Course Policies

AI: You are allowed to use AI like you use TAs. But we already have a TA and instructor who can help you with those questions. So we'd prefer you direct those questions to us instead.
That said, you are not allowed to use any generative AI to write assignments. This means no AI to create your slides, write a script for your presentations, write questions for the article, or write any part of your final project. If we find that you have used AI on assignments, you will get a 0 on that assignment. If your responses produce AI-like errors but I'm not confident that AI wrote them, I'll just grade them like usual. Any mistakes it makes (and it does make many mistakes) will be your mistakes.

Lateness: Late submissions for any assignments reduce the grade by 10% for each day that they are late. If you anticipate turning in an assignment late, please reach out to me in advance. I can't guarantee extensions on assignments, but we can try to find something that works for everyone.

Academic integrity: You are allowed to work together on the article summaries (and I'd be delighted if you did so), but please mention who you work with and what you have worked together on when you submit your assignments. Your writing needs to be your own. Same goes for the final. You are allowed to discuss articles together before class (again, I'd be delighted if you did so), but please come up with your own questions. You can't collaborate on the pre-lecture questions.

Accessibility: If you need academic adjustments or accommodations, please contact the Moses Center for Students with Disabilities at mosescsd@nyu.edu.

Electronics in class: Because this is a discussion-oriented class, we will try to create an atmosphere that minimizes distraction. This means that we will not allow laptops to be open for most of class. Lots of research documents that having technology that can access the internet or messaging has a huge negative impact on learning — both for the person using the technology and their neighbors (this is sometimes called technoference). So, no laptops. Ditto for phones. Take any notes you'd like on paper. I will also provide pdfs of the slides after each lecture so you can review them, but these won't contain all the information we discuss in class, so taking notes might still be helpful.

Course Schedule

Week 1

Monday, May 18: Syllabus + Intro to developmental cognitive science

This class will cover the goals and logistics of the course. The goal of the course is to gain a reasonable understanding of the methods, theories, and questions of interest in the field of developmental science. In the lecture, we'll look at some reasons for why developmental psychology is worth studying, and what we can hope to learn from it.

Required:
  • Course syllabus (this webpage)
Optional:
  • Carey, S. E. (2009). The Origin of Concepts. Oxford University Press.
    [Chapter 1].

Tuesday, May 19: Theories of development

This lecture will cover some theories of development, starting with classical nativist and empiricist debates from early modern philosophy, Piaget, and then moving onto more contemporary approaches to studying development. We'll aim to cover in broad strokes what each of these theories emphasizes, and the phenomena they care most about. In class, we will discuss some of the issues that arise in the book chapter.

Required:

  • Carey, S. E. (2009). The Origin of Concepts. Oxford University Press.
    [Ch. 2; about 35 pages]

Optional:

  • Slater et al. (1990). Size constancy at birth. Journal of Experimental Child Psychology, 49(2), 314-322.


Wednesday, May 21: Learning mechanisms

How should we think about learning? This lecture will focus on different kinds of learning mechanisms, and in particular on the domain-specificity or generality of learning. We will also talk about how perceptual learning differs from conceptual learning.

Required:
  • Emlen, S. (1975). The stellar orientation system of a migratory bird. Scientific American, 233, 102-111.
  • Saffran, J. R., Aslin, R. N., & Newport, E. L. (1996). Statistical learning by 8-month-old infants. Science, 274(5294), 1926-1928.

Week 2

Monday, May 25: Memorial Day; no class!


Tuesday, May 26: Perception and cognition in infancy

What do infant know about physical objects? We will explore some claims about early knowledge in infancy. We will also discuss some of the methods we use to study what infants know.

Required:
  • Spelke, E. S., Breinlinger, K., Macomber, J., & Jacobson, K. (1992). Origins of knowledge. Psychological Review, 99(4), 605–632.
  • Stahl, A. E., & Feigenson, L. (2015). Observing the unexpected enhances infants’ learning and exploration. Science, 348(6230), 91-94.

Wednesday, May 27: Core knowledge, empiricism, and constructivism

We will review how some of the different theories of development explain similar phenomena. We will as a case study the role of experience in how children understand others' actions.

Required:
  • Sommerville, J. A., Woodward, A. L., & Needham, A. (2005). Action experience alters 3-month-old infants' perception of others' actions. Cognition, 96(1), B1-B11.
  • Skerry, A. E., Carey, S. E., & Spelke, E. S. (2013). First-person action experience reveals sensitivity to action efficiency in prereaching infants. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 110(46), 18728-18733.
