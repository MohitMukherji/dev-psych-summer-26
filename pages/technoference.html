This page contains links to a few articles dealing with the impact of technology use in classrooms.

[1] An essay by Jonathan Haidt on why phones don't belong in classrooms.

[2] A scientific review of the effects of multitasking when it comes to texting and learning in the classroom.

[3] Another study showing how learning is greatly disrupted when technological distractors are present.

[4] Divided attention because of phones, laptops, and other smart gadgets reduces exam performance.

[5] Taking notes on laptops leads to worse in-class learning (and worse GPAs) than taking handwritten notes.


This is now already a huge literature, and I could have easily found and cited many tens of articles documenting similar effects.

To be transparent, I take most of my notes on my laptop so that I can search for stuff in my text files or so that I can easily share them with others to, e.g., provide feedback on talks. Laptops are great. But using them effectively in classrooms requires discipline to not surf the web or text or email others. And, from sitting in the back of many classrooms during my time as an undergrad, and from teaching this course last summer, I know that most of you aren't on your laptops for solely class-related reasons. These distractions will derail your own attention in class, but they also distract others around you, and they distract me because speaking to people who aren't listening to you is discouraging. So, we'll have a section of class where we put our laptops away and discuss the articles with each other, face-to-face, relying on only human intelligence (and memory) to communicate our ideas.
