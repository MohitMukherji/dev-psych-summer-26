The point of the reading responses is to get you to (1) engage with the readings deeply before we discuss them in class, and (2) identify what the relevant questions are. Therefore, response presentations will be graded based on the following rubric:

  1. Engagement with the article (5/10)
    Don't summarise the article. At most, have one slide that identifies key points and/or assumptions made in the article that are integral to your response.

    Your responses need to engage deeply with the central questions raised by the readings. For example, if the authors want to understand how infants perceive others' actions, your responses must in some way bear on how infants perceive others' actions. Focus on what issues are the most important or interesting for the authors, and what questions are still open. Don't list out a bunch of minor flaws or disconnected ideas. Instead, work through one idea in detail. Think about what next steps the science would need to take, and develop those ideas.

    Some ways you could do this are:

    • Critiquing the article. Lay out the argument briefly, identify a problem, and better still, suggest a solution. For example, your argument might go like this: "The authors argue that their experiment shows X. But that can't be right because Y. Y is true because of Z. Instead, the experiment that would show X is/what the researchers' experiment actually shows is ..."
    • Extending the idea to another domain. Connect the reading to another topic, spend some time developing that idea, and possibly propose an experiment that would test whether it applies and what it would show. Remember to tie it back to the current theory. For example, if you're interested in whether infants across different cultures expect others' actions to be efficient, think about what exactly it is about the different cultures that might have an effect on how infants understand actions, and what we would learn about infants' action understanding in general if we found cross-cultural differences.
    • Identify a methodological flaw in the experiment (this applies most clearly to empirical papers that we discuss, not review articles). Again, these can't just be that the sample sizes are too small—developmental research is hard. Instead, find flaws that will help advance the science in some meaningful way, and maybe suggest what you (or anyone with the means to run another experiment) could do to address them.

  2. Covering new ground (2/10)
    Your responses need to cover ground that hasn't been covered in the reading. If you're looking for ideas to consider for your response, one good place to look is in the discussion section of an empirical article, or a paragraph on open questions in review articles. You could develop these lines of thought further in your response.

  3. Engagement with questions (1.5/10)
    You will be graded on how well you are able to engage with the audience's questions, which may rely on previous papers that we have covered in class.

  4. Presentation clarity (1.5/10)
    You will be graded on the timeliness, clarity, and cohesiveness of your presentation (speaking and slide organization/structure). For example, stick within the time limit of ~5 minuetes. This may take some rehearsing before class.
